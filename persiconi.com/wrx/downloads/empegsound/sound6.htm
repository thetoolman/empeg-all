Balance Adjustment
We're just truckin' along on this thing aren't we?  As we all know, stereo was designed to mimic real life sound by dividing recording sound to separate two channels.   Because we use both of our (2) ears to judge where sound is coming from, stereo is pretty effective at recreating this.  With the technology available today, in a controlled environment, a properly mixed stereo signal does a very good job at building a 360° image in our mind.

We have one problem:  Our cars are not very good "controlled environments".  One of the main problems is that stereo was designed to have the listener an equal distance between the two speakers.   Yet because we sit so much closer to the left/front speaker, that speaker seems louder which in turn throws off the sound stage.  Luckily, we can fix that difference in volume with the Balance Control.

Note:  Adjustments made to the Balance Control benefit only the person in the driver's seat and will in fact result in worse sound for passengers.
 
Balance Calibration
While it is possible to conduct this test "by ear", I recommend picking up an SPL or level meter to measure the sounds coming from your system.  It's worth purchasing one since you'll need a meter if you plan on doing either the Manual or the Advanced Parametric EQ adjusts later in this tutorial.   They're also pretty useful for calibrating your home theater system.  In this tutorial, we'll use a RadioShack model with digital display (Part#: 33-2055).

1. Download pinknoisebothchannels.mp3 (Right-click, Save As..) and add it to your "empeg sound - Misc. Files" playlist.  Select this playlist and make sure it is marked "Ignore as Child" so that it won't get played during normal player use.

2.  Synchronize with your player and go find your car.

3. (Recommended: Drive your car to a quiet location.)  Take out your shiny new Sound Meter and turn it on.  Verify the following settings:

  • Set Weighting to C
  • Set Response to Slow
  • Range (i.e. 70-80dB) can be set to match your preferred playback volume.

4.  Insert your empeg into your car's sled and apply power. 

5.  Using the rotary controller (volume knob), access the player's Sound menu and apply the following settings:

  • Set Fader and Balance to their middle settings.
  • Set Loudness to 0.0dB.
  • Beep Level to 0%.
  • Pitchshift to 100% (3.00alpha software only).
  • Auto Volume Adjust to "Off" (3.00alpha software only).
  • Enter the Equalizer menu and select a preset #1 "Flat".

6.  Enter the Hijack menu by pressing and holding the rotary controller to apply the following settings: (If you do not have HiJack installed, you can skip this step).

  • Set Left/Right Time Alignment to "Off".
  • Set Tone: Bass Adjust and Tone: Treble Adjust to "Off".
  • Set Auto Volume Adjust to "Off".

7.  Begin playback of the Left/Right Pink Noise track while monitoring the levels picked up by the SPL meter.  You can ignore whatever value you get for the center channel.

8.  Tap the volume knob several times to bring up the Balance Control and adjust it to the right or left until you get the same dB value for both the Left and Right channel. 

  • If you're seated on the left, adjust the slider to the right (and vice versa).
  • Hold the SPL meter near the headrest of the driver's seat.

9.  Write down this setting and save it either on a piece of paper or in a .txt file stored on your computer.
 

Now that we've fixed the difference in volume between the left and right speakers, we still have one problem to tackle: The fact that the sound from the Left speaker reaches your ear a fraction of a second faster than the sound from the Right speaker.
