Amplifier Gain

Adjusting the gain control on your amplifier is probably the easiest tweak you can make to your sound system, but it is also one of the most important.  The goal is to set the gain on your amplifier only as loud as you need it without creating distortion.  Some advantages to having a properly calibrated amplifier Gain Control include:

  • Cleaner, non-distorted sound with a high signal-to-noise ratio.
  • Decreased chance of amplifying electrical noise that enters your system.
  • Prolonged life for your amplifier due to lower operating temperatures.

Preparation
Before we begin, we need to connect to your player so we can limit the player's output to prevent distortion.   We do this by capping its maximum volume at 0.0dB. 

1. Launch emplode and connected to your player.

2. Select "File > Configure Player" or click on the icon to bring up the following menu:

3. Click on the "Volume" tab and put a check in the box next to "Cap volume at 0dB." 

4. Synchronize and close emplode.

This setting prevents the player from playing anything louder than 0dB (the player uses negative numbers to represent anything below maximum output.)  Without this setting, the player is capable of sending a distorted or clipped signal to the amplifier.  That's bad.
 

Setting Amplifier Gain
Contrary to popular belief (and mine before writing this) the amplifier's gain control does not adjust the amplifier's power output.  Instead, the gain control on all amplifiers adjusts the input sensitivity of the amp to match the headunit.  Because aftermarket headunits have output voltages ranging from 200mV to 5 volts, amps need to compensate for this.   The gain control simply matches the amplifier's gain to the output level of the headunit.  Turning the input gain up does NOT indicate more power. Just more noise.  Remember, the input gain control has nothing to do with the power output of the amplifier, just how rapidly it will get loud.   Let's get started.

1.  Turn the gain control on the amplifier all the way down (counter-clockwise) to zero. If you have more than one amp in your system, perform this setup for each of them, one at a time.   Make sure only the amp you are calibrating is connected.

2.  If your amplifier has any EQ enhancements or Bass Boost features, turn these off.  Refer to your specific owner's manual for instructions.

3.  Continuing to refer to your owners manual.. If your amplifier has a built-in crossover:

  • Set the crossover to "Full Range/FR" if you do not have a separate subwoofer.
  • Set the crossover to "High Pass/HP" if you do have a separate subwoofer.
     
    • If your crossover is adjustable, try to match the crossover on the amp so that it overlaps with the crossover on the sub.  You can guess for now.

4.  Insert your player into your car's sled and apply power. 

5. Using the rotary controller (volume knob), access the player's Sound menu and apply the following settings:

  • Set Fader and Balance to their middle settings.
  • Set Loudness to 0.0dB.
  • Beep Level to 0%.
  • Pitchshift to 100% (3.00alpha software only).
  • Auto Volume Adjust to "Off" (3.00alpha software only).
  • Enter the Equalizer menu and select a preset #1 "Flat".

6.  Enter the Hijack menu by pressing and holding the rotary controller to apply the following settings: (If you do not have HiJack installed, you can skip this step).

  • Set Left/Right Time Alignment to "Off".
  • Set Tone: Bass Adjust and Tone: Treble Adjust to "Off".
  • Set Auto Volume Adjust to "Off".

7.  Select a "loud" but clean song and begin playing it.  Anything with distorted instruments or a poor recording will not work for this test.

8.  Turn the player's volume all the way up to 0.0dB.

9.  Slowly turn up the front gain control (clockwise) on the amplifier.  Keep increasing the gain until you either a) hear distortion from your speakers or b) the volume is as loud as you'd ever want to hear it.  IMPORTANT:  If you set the gain louder than you will ever use it, you are lowering your signal-to-noise ratio.  Play it safe.  It's better to keep the gain low.  You can always raise it later.

10.  Slowly turn up the rear gain control on the amplifier.  Keep increasing the gain until you can just barely detect the presence of the rear speakers while sitting in the driver's seat.  This is called "rear fill" by all those car audio geeks.

11.  You're done!  If you have more than one amp, you can move onto the next one.

Tony recommends turning the volume down at this point before you hurt your ears.  In my experience, this is also the point where your neighbors begin to lose patience.
 

See, that was pretty easy.  It took longer for you to read it and WAY longer for me to write than it takes to perform!   Now that we have the amplifier's gain control set, we can make sure your speakers have the same polarity.
