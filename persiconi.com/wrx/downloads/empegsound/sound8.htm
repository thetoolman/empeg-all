Parametric EQ Adjustment

The empeg's Parametric EQ is its most powerful sound related feature.  Yet, it is also one of its least used features.  Whereas the Time Alignment feature may go unused by most users because they simply don't know it exists, the Parametric EQ goes unused because most people are afraid of it.

There are two goals when using an equalizer: 1) To try to give your sound system a flat frequency response and 2) To build upon that "flat" response and tailor the sound to your liking.

What makes the Parametric EQ so powerful is its versatility.  They allow you to specify which frequency to boost or cut as well as the range of frequencies above or below that.   In contrast, Graphic EQs, the most common in car audio, only let your boost or cut a predefined range of frequencies.   This flexibility is what scares some people away from using the Parametric EQ on their empeg.  In this tutorial, we will walk you through the parametric EQ, step-by-step.

Parametric EQ
Just like with the Balance Control, you will need an SPL or level meter for this part.  As before, we'll use a RadioShack model with digital display (Part#: 33-2055).

1. Download the 10 required test tones and add them to a new "empeg sound - Test Tones" playlist.  The files are sorted alphabetically in the order that they are to be played.  Select this playlist and make sure it is marked "Ignore as Child" so that it won't get played during normal player use.  Synchronize with your player.

2.  Grab a pen and paper then go find your car.

3. (Recommended: Drive your car to a quiet location.)  Take out your shiny new Sound Meter and turn it on.  Verify the following settings:

  • Set Weighting to C
  • Set Response to Slow
  • Range (i.e. 70-80dB) can be set to match your preferred playback volume.

4.  Insert your empeg into your car's sled and apply power. 

5.  Using the rotary controller (volume knob), access the player's Sound menu and apply the following settings:

  • Make sure Balance is set to the same value you set earlier.
  • Set Fader to the middle.
  • Set Loudness to 0.0dB.
  • Beep Level to 0%.
  • Pitchshift to 100% (3.00alpha software only).
  • Auto Volume Adjust to "Off" (3.00alpha software only).
  • Enter the Equalizer menu and select an available preset (use Preset #2 if free) that has all bands set to 0.
  • Still in the Equalizer menu, make sure "Set Locks" is set to "Lock left=right".

6.  Enter the Hijack menu by pressing and holding the rotary controller to apply the following settings: (If you do not have Hijack installed, you can skip this step).

  • Set Left/Right Time Alignment to the same value you set earlier.
  • Set Tone: Bass Adjust and Tone: Treble Adjust to "Off".
  • Set Auto Volume Adjust to "Off".
7.  Grab that piece of paper and make a numbered list, 1 through 10.  This is where you will write down your readings for the ten test tones.

8.  Begin playback of the "empeg sound - Test Tones" playlist with the volume set to a comfortable listening level (-20dB is recommended).  Have your SPL meter turned on and held near your listening position.  You may need to sit in the passenger seat and hold the SPL meter where your head would normally be.

9. As the tracks play, you will have ten seconds to get a reading with the SPL meter and write your findings before the next track begins.  Your list should look something like this:

  • Sample Readings
  • Band 1 / 50Hz:        63dB
  • Band 2 / 100Hz:      75dB
  • Band 3 / 180Hz:      72dB
  • Band 4 / 350Hz:      74dB
  • Band 5 / 700Hz:      72dB
  • Band 6 / 1.30kHz:   73dB
  • Band 7 / 2.50kHz:   67dB
  • Band 8 / 5.00kHz:   68dB
  • Band 9 / 10.0kHz:   63dB
  • Band 10 / 18.0kHz: 64dB

10.  Because the SPL meter's frequency response is not perfect, we now need to correct our numbers.

  • Corrections
  • Band 1:  +2dB      63dB + 2dB = 65dB
  • Band 2:  +2dB      75dB + 2dB = 77dB
  • Band 3:   -1dB      72dB  - 1dB = 71dB
  • Band 4:  +0dB      74dB + 0dB = 74dB
  • Band 5:  +0dB      72dB + 0dB = 72dB
  • Band 6:  +0dB      73dB + 0dB = 73dB
  • Band 7:   -2dB      67dB  - 2dB = 65dB
  • Band 8:   -2dB      68dB  - 2dB = 66dB
  • Band 9:   -1dB      63dB  - 1dB = 62dB
  • Band 10:  0dB      64dB + 0dB = 64dB

11.  Next, add the highest reading found in Step 9 to the lowest reading and divide by 2.  This number number will be your target value.  In our example, that would be (77dB + 62dB) / 2 = 69.5dB.  Let's round that down to 69dB. 

12.  To complete our math exercise, take the corrected value for each band in Step 10 and subtract it from your target value.  This will give you the adjustment you'll need to make to each band.  Using our number in our example:

  • Target Value - Reading) = Adjustment
  • Band 1 / 50Hz: 69dB - 65dB =       +4dB
  • Band 2 / 100Hz: 69dB - 77dB =      -8dB
  • Band 3 / 180Hz:  69dB - 71dB =     -2dB
  • Band 4 / 350Hz: 69dB - 74dB =      -5dB
  • Band 5 / 700Hz: 69dB - 72dB =      -3dB
  • Band 6 / 1.30kHz: 69dB - 73dB =    -4dB
  • Band 7 / 2.50kHz: 69dB - 65dB =   +4dB
  • Band 8 / 5.00kHz: 69dB - 66dB =   +3dB
  • Band 9 / 10.0kHz: 69dB - 62dB =   +7dB
  • Band 10 / 18.0kHz: 69dB - 64dB = +5dB

13. Using the adjustment values you found in Step 12, adjust your EQ accordingly.  After you've made your adjustments, you may wish to try steps 8 through 12 a few more times to really fine tune your setup.  If you getting sporadic readings for one or two frequency bands, you may want to review the Phase Test section to make sure that one of your speakers (or even a tweeter if you have separates) isn't wired incorrectly.  Tip: If you prefer to do these adjustments outside of the car, use Hijack's "Force AC/DC Mode" option to force the player into DC mode.  Now you'll have access to all of the EQ modes that are reserved for in-car use.

  • Some people have expressed concerns over the fact that this tutorial asks you to raise and lower the frequency bands instead of only using negative values for each band.  While I agree that raising any digital signal above 0dB can cause clipping, there was a reason I did an equal amount of raising and lowering the values.  It ends up that applying too much gain to any band (either negative or positive gain) causes the neighboring bands to be affected as well.  Because each band has a curved shape, raising band 5 by 10dB would also cause bands 4 and 6 to be raised by a few dB (and the inverse would happen if you lowered it by 10dB).  Because of this, it is best to keep the changes as minimal as possible by finding a "target value" as we did in Step 12.
  • Because your EQ now has positive values (up to +7dB in our example), it is important to be careful as you raise the players volume close to 0dB.  You may experience clipping.  To avoid this, you may wish to redo the Amp Gain part of this tutorial with a lower volume than the 0dB we used.  (In our example, we'd set the amp gain with the volume set to -7dB).  This will reduce the temptation to ever raise the volume so high you'd experience clipping.  I know it's not a pretty workaround, but it does "work".

14. In the last thirteen steps, we've done our best to give your sound system a flat frequency response.  While a "flat" frequency response aims to compensate for any irregularities in your setup, you may be surprised that it doesn't sound quite the way you thought it would.  This is because most of us prefer a frequency response that is more "curved" like a smiley face (higher values for the low and high frequencies.)

  • After copying your EQ settings to another Preset (this is a menu option, you don't need to do this manually), add a few dB to bands 1 through 3 as well as bands 8 through 10.  At the same time, you can cut a few dB from the middle bands, 4 through 7.  The goal is to find a sound that you like best.  This will most certainly depend on the type of music you're playing and will take some practice to find a setting you like.   If you goof up, you can always go back to your EQ Preset that you did not alter.
  • You may wish to play with the empeg's Loudness Control (found in the Sound menu).  This feature adds the "smiley face" curve automatically.  The best part about this option is that as you raise the volume, the empeg applies less and less of that smiley curve to your signal.  This helps to prevent clipping while still making the music sound better at lower volumes.
  • Important: Do not use Hijack's Bass and Treble controls.  While these controls are very handy, they are only for an empeg that does not have custom Parametric EQ settings.  This is because Hijack operates  by "borrowing" bands 9 and 10 from your EQ to work its magic.  So, once you start using the Bass and Treble controls, your EQ adjustments will have been a waste.
     

Congratulations!  You have successfully completed the tutorial and helped get the most out of your empeg!
